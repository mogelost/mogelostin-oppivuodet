# This is not just a question of jobs

The technological shift triggered by generative AI may differ from earlier ones. Not necessarily in its elements, but in its force. It is not merely a new machine or software tool replacing human labor: we have only witnessed the overture to a change that extends efficiency into fields we have been accustomed to seeing as distinctly human domains of work.

Most societies have built their order on the assumption that people earn their place by selling their labor and receiving a livelihood in return. Work is not only about money; it is also a source of legitimacy, social standing, and a sense of meaning.

But what if that basic assumption begins to crack? If AI lowers the market value of many kinds of work, the current model — in which the experience of belonging is tied to work — will be put under strain. We are not claiming that this will certainly happen, nor are we predicting an apocalypse. We are only saying that it is more honest to acknowledge the possibility and think through alternatives in advance than to continue assuming that “the markets will always take care of it.”

## The strengths and limits of the market economy

The market economy, economic growth, and international trade have historically been powerful drivers of prosperity and poverty reduction. According to the World Bank, the growing participation of developing countries in global trade has coincided with a dramatic decline in extreme poverty, and over the long term extreme poverty has fallen substantially in the post-1990 world.[1][2]

That does not mean markets are perfect. They generate innovation, growth, and efficiency, but also disruption, pressure to adapt, and inequality. Welfare states and various forms of social protection did not emerge out of nowhere, but in response to the fact that market logic alone does not guarantee people income, health, or social inclusion.[3]

The Nordic countries are a well-known example that markets and social protection do not have to be opposites. In the Nordic model, a strong public sector, extensive welfare services, and a social safety net coexist with the market economy.[4]

## Technology and work: adapting through creation and destruction

History shows that technology can raise living standards and productivity while also dismantling old structures. This duality is not new. Technological change often creates a transition period in which some jobs disappear, others change, and new ones emerge in their place.[5]

According to an analysis by the C.D. Howe Institute, automation does not inevitably lead to mass unemployment. Its effects depend on whether new technology replaces human labor or complements it. The report suggests that past development points more toward a gradual restructuring of tasks than toward an immediate collapse of the labor market as a whole.[5]

That does not make the transition painless. Even if the economy adjusts in the long run, in the short and medium term it can easily produce friction, uncertainty, and political tension. That is precisely why technological disruption is not merely a technical issue, but also a social and moral one.

## Economic dependencies and a basic promise

If the price of labor falls significantly, a tension may emerge in the system: production grows, but purchasing power and consumption do not necessarily follow. In that case, the problem would not only be the quantity of production, but how people gain access to the wealth being produced.

One way to think about this is to distinguish between two layers. The first would be the market layer, where money would still measure choices, risk, ambition, and added value. The second would be a basic promise: a minimum level that belongs to a person as a member of society, regardless of their momentary market value.

At the same time, it is worth noting that the same disruption that makes such a basic promise more necessary may also make it more feasible. A common objection to basic income or stronger basic security is that it would simply be too expensive. But if AI and automation really do lower the cost of labor, increase productivity, and make the production of basic necessities cheaper than before, then society’s material capacity to secure a minimum baseline may also grow. In that case, the issue is not merely adding a new expense on top of the old system, but asking whether the efficiency created by technological disruption can be used in a new way to distribute its benefits more broadly.

This would not mean “free cars for everyone,” but rather a basic foundation on which a human life can be built: housing, food, health, energy, and a connection to society. The idea is not historically alien. The core idea of the welfare state is precisely that the state, or the wider network of institutions tied to it, protects and advances the economic and social well-being of its citizens.[3] However market-friendly one may be, not all social functions belong to the market — and even when they do, markets do not always produce the best outcome.

The disruption may not only force the question of a basic promise — it may also, for the first time, make it realistic at scale. According to Britannica, the welfare state typically includes not only social insurance but also the public provision of basic services such as education, healthcare, and sometimes housing.[3] In the Nordic model, this appears in the form of extensive services and safety nets that are based not only on individual market success but also on need and shared responsibility.[4]

## Scenarios

If AI begins to weaken the connection between work and livelihood on a broad scale, society faces at least three possible directions. None of them is entirely without problems, but laying them out helps us see that this is not only a technological shift but also a matter of political and moral choice.

### 1. Continued hardening

The current system remains largely intact. Jobs change, but the underlying structures are not updated. In that case, part of the population may lose its position, and social tensions may intensify. The market economy may still generate wealth, but in an increasingly uneven way.

### 2. A control-based solution

Support is expanded, but so are supervision, conditions, and administrative control. The system may provide stability, but at the cost of reduced autonomy and greater dependence on bureaucratic mechanisms.

### 3. A layered update

The minimum conditions of human dignity are detached more clearly from work. The basic promise secures a baseline, while the market layer continues to function in familiar ways. People can still pursue added value, wealth, status, and ambitious goals, but fundamental security would no longer depend solely on labor-market viability.

This third alternative does not abolish markets or the importance of work. What it changes above all is what society considers a non-negotiable minimum, and what it leaves to the market.

## Could a basic promise help solve the problems brought by this transition?

The idea of a basic promise can also be understood as something that stabilizes the market economy, not merely restricts it. If a large share of people lose their livelihood or their sense of participation at the same time as productive capacity grows, the legitimacy of the system begins to weaken. Markets require not only production, but also demand, trust, and social stability.

From this perspective, a basic promise would not be the opposite of the market economy, but rather a kind of stabilizing foundation beneath it. The market layer could still measure choices, risk, entrepreneurship, and added value. What would change is that the minimum conditions of human dignity would no longer be wholly dependent on labor-market fitness.

## Challenges and possibilities

Such an idea understandably raises the question of incentives: what happens to work if the essentials of life are guaranteed anyway?

There is no final answer to that. Every generation has, in its own way, worried about lazy or ill-behaved youth. But at the very least, not all empirical evidence supports the simple assumption that security automatically destroys motivation. In the evaluation of Finland’s basic income experiment, the overall employment effects were small, but recipients of the basic income reported greater well-being, less mental strain, and a more positive experience of economic security than the control group.[6][7]

This does not prove that any particular model would solve the problems of the future. It does, however, show that income security cannot be assessed through a single question alone — namely, whether it immediately increases employment or not. It may also affect people’s functioning, trust, and sense that life is manageable.[7]

But the objections are not only about incentives. It is at least as important to ask what kinds of losses the current system already produces, even if they are not always counted as economic costs.

## Untapped potential is also an economic problem

One of the most common objections is the fear that some people would take advantage of a stronger basic level of security. The concern is not groundless, and no system eliminates it entirely. From a macroeconomic perspective, however, the more important question is one of scale: which is the greater risk — limited abuse, or a system that produces widespread uncertainty, exclusion, and hopelessness?

If a growing number of people are left permanently outside the circles of work, livelihood, and meaningful participation, the consequences do not appear only in individual lives. They also appear in social stability, public health, trust, and long-term productivity. In that sense, it may be the lesser evil that some people occasionally receive more from the system than they “deserve,” rather than allowing the whole system to push an increasing number of people toward a life without horizons.

The discussion often focuses on how many people would stop working if basic security were strengthened. But it is at least as important to ask what might be released into view. The current system directs a great many people to spend most of their energy on work that does not match their abilities, interests, or potential. In macroeconomic terms, that too is inefficiency: not only unused labor, but unused human capacity.

It is difficult to measure how much society has lost because, for decades, people have had to direct their abilities first and foremost toward securing a livelihood rather than toward the things through which they might create the most value for themselves, for others, or for society. Still, it is reasonable to assume that the loss is large. When people are bound more tightly to survival than to meaningful activity, creativity, initiative, learning, care, community projects, and other forms of value that are harder to measure — but no less real — remain unrealized.

So the question is not only how to prevent passivity. It is also how much value we lose under the current model, which forces a large share of people to spend their lives primarily proving their economic worthiness. If more people had a real opportunity to direct their time toward their strengths and toward things they experience as meaningful, the result could be not only greater well-being but also more long-term social and economic value.

## Reflection: can markets withstand the solution?

If AI transforms work, the biggest question may not simply be how many tasks disappear. A more fundamental question is how society defines participation, value, and basic security in a situation where markets may no longer distribute livelihood through work as broadly as before.

The economy may adapt, as it has adapted before.[5] But it is not self-evident that adaptation will happen fairly or without major tensions. For that reason, it is plausible that alongside technological disruption, we will also have to think about new mechanisms of income distribution, services, and basic security.

So this is not only a question of what AI does to work. It is also a question of what society decides to do with human dignity.

## Conclusions

In this text, we do not claim to know the final solution. We are not offering a utopian magic formula. But we do suggest that the old assumption — that all value must be earned by selling one’s labor — should be questioned more seriously than before.

Markets have historically been exceptionally effective at creating prosperity and reducing poverty.[1][2] At the same time, welfare states emerged precisely because societies recognized the limits of markets in certain fundamental areas.[3][4] Technological disruption may make that tension more visible than before.

So the central question may not be whether to choose markets or social protection, but how to rethink their relationship in a world where the connection between work and livelihood may weaken. One possible direction is the **basic promise + market layer** model proposed here. It is not the only option, but it is one way of taking the disruption seriously without giving up either the strengths of markets or the minimum demands of human dignity.
